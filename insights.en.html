Opinion · Commentary · Standpoint

Insights

On the front page we soberly count who comes last. Here we say why. Our own research, our own sources – but without the diplomatic voice. Because last place rarely falls from the sky. Usually, someone is responsible for it.

pointed partisan fact-based read from the left
⚠Hinweis: This page is the opinion section of Schlusslicht. These texts are commentary and satire – pointed and partisan, but each verified against its own real, linked source. If you prefer it neutral, read the front page.

Last place is not a natural disaster. Nobody is born unhappy, no country is born poor, no industry pays starvation wages by nature. Behind every last-placed entry is a chain of decisions – and almost always, those who made them have never stood at the back themselves. That is exactly what we talk about here.

Standpoint · Nuclear waste

The Barrel That Outlives Us

France reported 56 nuclear reactors in operation in 2025, and its long-term radioactive waste program is still built around materials that remain hazardous for far longer than any election cycle.[1]

That is the old republican bargain in its purest form: today’s electricity, tomorrow’s burden, and a polite promise that some future generation will inherit the invoices, the geology, and the caution tape.[1]

The political comfort lies in calling the problem technical, because technical problems can be outsourced to engineers, while the moral question — who gets to enjoy the power and who gets to live with the residue — is harder to bury.[1]

Governments should bind every new reactor or waste expansion to a fully funded, publicly supervised decommissioning reserve, so the cost sits with the operator from the start rather than drifting into the lap of taxpayers and children.[1]

France still runs a large reactor fleet, which means waste is not a theory but a standing political debt.

Standpoint · Shipping

The Sea Has a Smokestack

International shipping has carried about 80% of world trade by volume in recent years, and its emissions have made it one of the most consequential drivers of climate damage while remaining largely invisible to consumers.[2]

The ocean has become the world’s cheapest hiding place: the goods arrive, the exhaust disappears over the horizon, and the climate bill comes back to shore dressed as someone else’s problem.[2]

The uncomfortable truth is that maritime trade thrives on weak regulation, flag-shopping, and a political habit of praising global commerce while pretending its fuel burns nowhere in particular.[2]

Policy should impose a steep, predictable carbon price on shipping fuel and direct the revenue into port electrification and zero-emission vessels, because clean logistics should not depend on the goodwill of the dirtiest market participants.[2]

Most world trade still moves by sea, which makes shipping emissions a hidden mass problem.

Standpoint · Biodiversity

The Quiet Unemployment of Nature

The Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services found that 1 million species are threatened with extinction, a figure large enough to describe not a crisis at the margin but a systematic collapse of living infrastructure.[3]

That is what ecological collapse looks like before it arrives fully dressed: not thunder, but bookkeeping — species removed, habitats stripped, and the political class calling the wreckage a regrettable side effect.[3]

The damage is not accidental; it follows land use, industrial agriculture, extraction, and infrastructure built on the assumption that every meadow, wetland, and forest is merely vacant development waiting for a logo.[3]

Public money should be redirected from habitat-destroying subsidies toward restoration contracts with binding species targets, because conservation without budgetary teeth is only ceremonial concern.[3]

1 million species
The extinction risk is so broad that it reaches the foundation of ecosystems, not just a few emblematic animals.

The house rules, from the back

  1. Last place is made, not given. We always ask: by whom?
  2. Numbers are not neutral. Whoever collects, sorts and publishes them makes a choice.
  3. We take sides – for those at the very back, against those who keep them there.
  4. We attack power, not people. Structures, not the vulnerable.
  5. And we stay verifiable: every pointed claim rests on a source linked up front.
