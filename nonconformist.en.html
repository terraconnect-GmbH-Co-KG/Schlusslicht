Essay 01

The Illusion of Earned Wealth

Our society glorifies the myth that wealth is solely the fruit of individual labor, yet history reveals that most capital is inherited through lineage and structural advantage. The concept of meritocracy obscures how initial endowments determine the trajectory of a life before any effort is exerted.

We must distinguish between the dignity of work and the illusion that work alone generates the means of existence without the assistance of prior accumulation. Inheritance is not a passive gift but an active mechanism of class continuity that predates the current worker's contribution.

A truly equitable system would recognize that the 'self-made' individual is a philosophical fiction, relying on the unacknowledged labor of ancestors and the unpaid labor of the marginalized. Acknowledging this debt is the first step toward dismantling the feudal structures hidden within modern capitalism.

Lines of thought: Marx on capital accumulation, Piketty on inheritance, Gramsci on hegemony

Essay 02

Tools That Rule Their Makers

Technology is rarely neutral; it is the material embodiment of the will to power that shapes human behavior to serve the efficiency of the machine rather than the needs of the human. We have built systems that optimize for engagement and profit, effectively colonizing our attention and autonomy.

The machine does not serve the human; the human serves the algorithm. Herrschaft is no longer wielded by a king but by the code that dictates our reality.

True liberation requires a refusal to let technical rationality dictate the scope of our moral and political possibilities. We must reclaim the capacity to design tools that enhance human flourishing rather than merely extracting value from it.

Until we recognize technology as a political force, we remain subjects of a system that views us as data points rather than as beings with inherent dignity. The path forward lies in subverting the logic of efficiency to serve the logic of care.

Lines of thought: Heidegger on technology, Marcuse on one-dimensional man, Foucault on discipline

Essay 03

Schools for Subservience

Modern education systems often function as factories for conformity, training students to adapt to the existing economic order rather than to question its foundations. The curriculum prioritizes standardized performance over critical inquiry, ensuring that the workforce remains compliant and predictable.

Education is either a brake on human potential or a hammer for liberation; it cannot be both. The current system is a brake.

Democratic education must prioritize the development of critical consciousness, allowing students to recognize and challenge the structures that limit their freedom. This approach transforms the classroom into a space of political struggle and intellectual awakening.

Only when education ceases to be a tool for adaptation and becomes a practice of freedom can it fulfill its promise of human development. We must demand a pedagogy that empowers the oppressed to name their world and change it.

Lines of thought: Dewey on democracy, Freire on pedagogy of the oppressed, Bourdieu on cultural capital

Essay 04

Fear as the Foundation of Order

Powers maintain control not merely through force but by manufacturing a pervasive sense of insecurity that keeps the populace dependent on their protection. This strategic anxiety redirects attention from structural injustices to the immediate threat of chaos or the outsider.

Fear is the invisible chain that binds the citizen to the state. Without the threat, the leash is cut.

The political utility of fear relies on the inability of the individual to trust in their own capacity for safety and solidarity. Herrschaft thrives on the isolation of the anxious subject who cannot imagine a future without the protector.

To dismantle this instrument of control, we must cultivate collective courage and the ability to face uncertainty without the promise of authoritarian security. True freedom begins when we refuse to trade our autonomy for the illusion of safety.

Lines of thought: Arendt on totalitarianism, Spinoza on fear, Hobbes on the state of nature

Essay 05

Democracy Beyond the Ballot

The conventional definition of democracy is limited to the periodic act of voting, which reduces political participation to a passive endorsement of pre-selected elites. This narrow view ignores the daily practices of collective decision-making that occur in communities, workplaces, and public spaces.

Voting is a ritual of submission, not an act of power. Democracy is the daily refusal to let strangers decide your life.

A robust democracy must extend into the economy and the social sphere, where power is most concentrated and where the majority is most excluded. This requires structures of direct participation that allow people to govern their own conditions of life.

We must reimagine democracy as a continuous practice of self-determination rather than a discrete event managed by the state. The goal is to create institutions where the people are the authors of their own laws and the masters of their own future.

Lines of thought: Pateman on participatory democracy, Holloway on revolutionary change, assemblies as power
