Essay 01

The Inheritance of Unearned Merit

The modern concept of performance ignores the foundational role of inheritance, treating wealth and status as the sole result of individual effort. This illusion masks how family assets, social networks, and cultural capital determine outcomes before any personal work begins. By ignoring these preconditions, society legitimizes inequality as the natural result of merit rather than the artifact of historical advantage.

Merit is a myth that disguises inheritance as effort.

True justice requires acknowledging that no one builds from scratch; every achiever stands on a foundation laid by others. A radical critique of capitalism must therefore target not just wages but the unearned privileges that distort the entire system of distribution[1].

We must redefine performance to include the debt owed to the past and the obligation to the future, dismantling the myth of self-made success.

Lines of thought: Marx on endemic faults, Gramsci on cultural capital, Raworth on inheritance

Essay 02

Tools That Rule Their Makers

Technology was promised as the liberation of human will, yet it has become the primary mechanism of domination in modern life. Algorithms dictate behavior, surveillance normalizes control, and automated systems erode human autonomy under the guise of efficiency. The tool no longer serves the user; the user serves the system, becoming a data point in a machine that knows more than it reveals.

We did not master technology; we became its modules.

Herrschaft through technology is not a bug but a feature of capitalist rationality, where control is optimized for profit rather than human dignity[1]. This structural domination requires a critique that goes beyond reform to challenge the very logic of technological progress as it currently exists.

Freedom demands a technology that answers to human needs rather than market metrics, returning agency to the collective rather than the algorithm.

Lines of thought: Marxists on systemic critique, Foucault on surveillance, Fisher on capitalist time

Essay 03

Schools for Obedience

Education today functions less as a path to liberation than as a factory for ideological conformity, training individuals to fit into existing economic hierarchies. Curricula emphasize compliance, standardized testing, and vocational over critical inquiry, creative thought, or civic engagement. The system produces workers who can follow instructions but cannot question the orders they receive.

Schools teach us to kneel before the market, not to stand against it.

True education must be a practice of freedom, cultivating the capacity to imagine alternatives and resist the arbitrary authority of economic and political systems[2]. Without this radical shift, schooling remains a tool of adaptation that reinforces scarcity and intellectual poverty rather than overcoming them[2].

We need an education that awakens the imagination to dismantle the prison of the present and build a world beyond capital.

Lines of thought: Gramsci on hegemony, Arendt on education, Anarchist Library on democratic governance

Essay 04

The Fear That Buys Consent

Fear is the silent currency of modern governance, exchanged daily for security, stability, and the illusion of safety. Political elites weaponize anxiety about crime, terrorism, and economic collapse to justify authoritarian measures that would otherwise be rejected by the public. This manufactured dread ensures compliance, transforming citizens into passive subjects who traded freedom for the promise of protection.

Fear is the price we pay to avoid thinking.

When fear becomes the primary instrument of control, democracy shrinks into a managed panic where the only choice is between different forms of surrender[1]. A radical left strategy must expose this mechanism and replace fear with collective courage, challenging the system that spawns these grievances rather than accepting its reforms[1].

To reclaim freedom, we must learn to fear the system more than we fear the unknown, and organize against the architecture of dread.

Lines of thought: Bloch on fear, Arendt on tyranny, Marxists on endemic faults

Essay 05

Democracy Beyond the Ballot

The ballot has become a ritual of legitimation rather than a tool of power, offering a narrow choice between candidates who serve the same structural interests. Voting every few years does not grant genuine self-determination when the economy, media, and state operate beyond public control. This hollowed-out democracy masks the reality that true power lies in the boardrooms and algorithms, not the polling stations.

Voting is the ceremony that hides the abdication of power.

Democracy must be reimagined as a continuous practice of collective decision-making that transcends the electoral cycle and challenges the arbitrary authority of economic systems[2]. We need governance that does away with scarcity and promotes democratic control over the means of production, rather than just the means of representation[2].

Real democracy is not a event but a daily struggle to reclaim the commons from the logic of capital and return power to the people.

Lines of thought: Anarchist Library on democratic governance, Marxists on reform and revolution, Bloch on open society
