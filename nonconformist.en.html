Essay 01

The Weight of Unearned Excellence

Merit is often defined as the value of an action rooted in free conduct, effort, and orientation toward a good[2]. Yet societal recognition frequently conflates this earned excellence with natural talent or inherited privilege, undermining true justice[2]. When we reward outcomes without tracing them to personal initiative, we distort the ethical core of distributive fairness[2].

Talent is not merit; inheritance is not effort. The bank of mum and dad does not qualify as justice[2][4].

Aristotle argued that justice means giving people what they deserve in proportion to their merit, a concept of proportional equality[3]. Modern interpretations struggle to reconcile this with the reality of intergenerational wealth, where gifting collides with meritocratic ideals[4]. True justice requires distinguishing between earned contribution and unearned privilege[3].

If merit is the foundation of justice, then natural talent and inherited wealth must not be recognized as such[2]. We must demand that rewards trace back to the subject's own initiative and free activity[2]. Only then can we restore the autonomous, effortful nature that defines ethical value[2].

Aristotle, Pojman, proportional equality

Essay 02

The Silent Tyrant of Tools

Technology has evolved from a mere instrument into a system that shapes human behavior and reinforces existing hierarchies[2]. While we claim tools are neutral, they often embed the values of their creators, perpetuating power structures[2]. This hidden influence transforms technology into a form of governance that operates without explicit consent[2].

Tools are not free; they are chains. The code dictates the will[2].

Heinz suggested that technology acts as a form of rule, operating silently to enforce norms[2]. When technology becomes a matter of rule, it operates without explicit authority, enforcing standards[2]. This dynamic reveals how modern systems govern through technical means rather than political will[2].

If technology is rule, then it must be challenged, not just used. We must demand that technical means serve democratic will[2]. Only then can we restore the human agency that defines ethical value[2].

Heinz, critical theory, algorithmic governance
