Insights

On the front page we soberly count who comes last. Here we say why. Same data, same sources – but without the diplomatic voice. Because last place rarely falls from the sky. Usually, someone is responsible for it.

pointed partisan fact-based read from the left
⚠Hinweis: This page is the opinion section of Schlusslicht. These texts are commentary and satire – pointed and partisah, but verifiable against the front page's numbers. If you prefer neutral, read the front page.

Last place is not a natural disaster. Nobody is born unhappy, no country is born poor, no industry pays starvation wages by nature. Behind every last-placed entry is a chain of decisions – and almost always, those who made them have never stood at the back themselves. That is exactly what we talk about here.

Standpoint · Low wages

Rent Rises, Tips Stay Small

The legal minimum wage in Germany is 12.82 euros per hour, yet millions live at the limit.

Tips are not included, but rent still rises faster than wages.

Cleaners earn 13.50 euros, hairdressers 14.40, and seasonal farm workers often just the minimum.

Minimum wage must rise to match the cost of living in every region.

Standpoint · Rail delays

Delay Is the New Punctuality

Long-distance punctuality for Deutsche Bahn in 2024 was only 61.5 percent.

Delay is the new punctuality, and the target is set above 60 percent.

Total delay minutes reached plus 41 million, with an investment backlog of nearly 90 billion euros.

The rail network requires immediate investment to restore reliability.

Standpoint · Press freedom

Journalism Now Criminalized

The RSF World Press Freedom Index shows North Korea at rank 12, Syria at 15, and China at 14.

Journalism is being criminalized, and even democracies are joining in now.

42 countries are rated as having a 'very serious' situation for press freedom.

Governments must enact laws that protect journalists from criminalization.

Standpoint · Climate

First place stays empty — for the tenth time

The climate index keeps its top three ranks empty because no country does enough to earn them. One can dismiss that as sober methodology — or read it as what it is: a ten-year-long admission of failure.

"We can't afford climate action right now" is the most expensive sentence of the century. It will be paid by those who never said it.

While Saudi Arabia, Russia and Iran cement the bottom of the table, Germany merrily approves new motorway sections and misses its targets. The bill is paid not by the corporation making profits today but by the schoolgirl living with the consequences in 2050.

Climate policy is distribution policy. The question is not whether change costs something, but who pays — and so far the answer has reliably been: not those who could.

Standpoint · Transit

The bus that never comes

Millions of people in Germany have no reasonable access to public transport. In some villages a bus runs once or twice a day — if at all. Without a car, you are cut off. Literally.

Mobility is not a luxury. It decides who gets to work, to the doctor, into life. An empty timetable is a political statement.

Meanwhile the railway sits on an investment backlog of roughly 90 billion euros, and in May every third long-distance train was late. Japan's Shinkansen manages 99.7 percent punctuality. That is not physics — it is priorities.

Public services mean: the state guarantees the essentials for everyone — not only for those who could afford them privately anyway.

The house rules, from the back

  1. Last place is made, not given. We always ask: by whom?
  2. Numbers are not neutral. Whoever collects, sorts and publishes them makes a choice.
  3. We take sides – for those at the very back, against those who keep them there.
  4. We attack power, not people. Structures, not the vulnerable.
  5. And we stay verifiable: every pointed claim rests on a source linked up front.
