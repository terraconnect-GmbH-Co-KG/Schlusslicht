Essay 01

The Merit Trap

Meritocracy promises that success flows naturally from talent and effort, creating a world where the most capable rise to the top. This narrative insists that inequality is not unjust but deserved, a reflection of personal virtue rather than systemic bias [3].

Merit is not a measure of worth; it is a mask for power.

By framing advantage as the result of individual merit, the system obscures how childhood, class, and access to education predetermine outcomes long before any 'competition' begins. The promise of fairness becomes a tool to delegitimize demands for structural change [2].

To challenge meritocracy is not to reject effort, but to demand a world where effort is not the only path to dignity and where success is not hoarded as private property [2].

Lines of thought: Michael Sandel, Daniel Markovits, critical theory of inequality

Essay 02

The Invisible Foundation

Care work—raising children, tending the sick, supporting the elderly—sustains the entire economy yet remains systematically unpaid or underpaid. It is treated as a private duty, not a public necessity, and thus excluded from the metrics of productivity that drive capitalist growth [2].

Capitalism extracts life from those who give it freely.

This exclusion is not accidental but structural: by defining care as 'non-work,' the system ensures that the burden of human reproduction falls on the most vulnerable, disproportionately women and marginalized groups [2]. The labor that keeps society alive is rendered invisible to justify its exploitation.

Recognizing care as foundational labor demands a redefinition of value itself, one that places human well-being above profit and communal responsibility above individual accumulation [2].

Lines of thought: Nancy Fraser, Silvia Federici, Kathi Weeks

Essay 03

Democracy as Disruption

Modern democracy is often reduced to elections, parties, and procedures, as if participation alone guarantees freedom. Yet this formalism masks a deeper reality: democracy thrives not when it is managed, but when it disrupts the status quo [1].

Democracy is not the rule of the majority; it is the refusal of the powerless to be ruled.

When democracy becomes a tool for stabilizing hierarchy rather than exposing it, it ceases to be democratic. True democracy emerges in moments of collective action that refuse the limits imposed by elites and demand a reimagining of power itself [1].

To reclaim democracy is to recognize that its essence lies not in ballots but in the unyielding assertion that no one is entitled to rule without consent, and that consent must be continually renegotiated [1].

Lines of thought: Hannah Arendt, Jacques Rancière, John Dewey
