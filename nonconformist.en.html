Essay 01

The Architecture of the Ordinary

What we call normal is not a reflection of reality but a carefully maintained boundary that excludes dissent before it can articulate itself. Power does not need to shout to dominate; it thrives in the quiet assumption that the current order is the only possible one[1].

Normality is the silent gatekeeper that locks the future inside the present.

When alternatives are labeled aberrant, the system protects itself not through force but through the erosion of imagination. This is the work of hegemony: making the extractive logic of capitalism feel like common sense rather than a choice.

To break normality is not to seek chaos but to reclaim the capacity to see that the walls around us are painted, not built.

Lines of thought: Gramsci, Arendt, Foucault

Essay 02

The Colonization of the Present

Time under capitalism is not a neutral measure but a resource to be extracted, optimized, and sold until nothing remains for the self. The worker does not merely lose hours; they lose the texture of their own existence, reduced to a function of productivity[1].

Time is the first property stolen before the land is ever fenced.

This extraction creates a hollow present where the future is always owed to someone else. Life becomes a debt of minutes, each one accounted for, never truly owned.

To reclaim time is to refuse the ledger and insist on moments that cannot be priced, only lived.

Lines of thought: Fisher, Bloch, Marx

Essay 03

The Strength of the Shared

Solidarity is often mistaken for empathy, but it is far more rigorous: it is the active construction of shared jurisdiction over life. In a system that isolates individuals to weaken collective power, solidarity becomes the only viable form of resistance[1].

Solidarity is the act of making the private public and the individual collective.

Without it, reform remains a plea and revolution a fantasy. True solidarity does not ask for inclusion but builds the infrastructure of a new world from the ground up.

It is the practice of saying: your life is my life, and our freedom is not a gift but a shared claim.

Lines of thought: Raworth, Gramsci, Weeks
