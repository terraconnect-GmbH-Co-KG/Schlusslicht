Nonconformist

The other pages count and comment. This page thinks more fundamentally: about obedience, property, normality and the question of why we administer a world instead of changing it. Radical in substance, nonviolent in means, verifiable in thought.

Note: This is an explicitly labeled opinion and philosophy section (protected speech, Art. 5 of the German Basic Law). It targets structures and habits of thought, never individual persons. It calls for nothing except thinking for yourself.

—

Essay 01

Obedience is not a virtue

Children first learn to be good, and only later to be just — and most institutions would be happy if it stopped at the former. School rewards the right answer, not the right question. The workplace rewards availability, not dissent. The state calls it order when nobody makes trouble, and forgets to mention whom that order feeds and whom it merely administers.

History's great crimes were rarely committed by the disobedient. Almost always by people who merely did as they were told.

That does not mean every rule is wrong. It means no rule becomes right simply by being one. Whoever obeys should be able to say, at any moment, why — and who benefits. If they cannot, their obedience is not a virtue but a convenience dressed up as one.

The nonconformist is not the one who always says no. It is the one who has not automated the yes.

Lines of thought: Arendt, La Boétie ("voluntary servitude"), the Milgram experiments.

Essay 02

Property entails obligations. For whom, exactly?

Germany's constitution contains a sentence so radical you might mistake it for a misprint: "Property entails obligations. Its use shall also serve the public good." Article 14, paragraph 2. Not a pamphlet, not a manifesto — constitutional law.

And yet we live as if it said the opposite: as if property were a natural right to consequence-free ownership. As if the empty apartment were a private matter, land speculation a hobby, and inherited wealth an achievement of the heir.

Nobody earned the home they were born into — neither the poor one nor the rich one. Whoever says merit must lower their voice when inheritance comes up.

The question is not whether people may own things. The question is at what point ownership begins to govern other people's lives — their rent, their work, their time. From that point on, property is no longer a private matter but a question of power. And in a democracy, questions of power belong on the table, not in the land registry.

Lines of thought: Art. 14(2) of the German Basic Law, Proudhon (as a question, not a slogan), Piketty.

Essay 03

Normality is an invention — and somebody profits from it

"That's just how it is." Five words that have prevented more than any censorship. The forty-hour week did not fall from the sky, nor did our concept of merit, nor the logic of exploitation, nor even the idea that a life should resemble a résumé. All of it was made — by people, with interests, at a particular time.

What was made can be made differently. That is not daydreaming, that is a history lesson: the free Sunday, universal suffrage, the abolition of child labor — every one of these self-evident things was once an outrage to those who lived off the old normality.

Whoever tells you there is no alternative is not describing the world. They are describing their dividend.

The first step out of normality is unspectacular: treat it as a claim instead of a fact. Ask who benefits from it. And stop mistaking your own exhaustion for personal failure when it is, in truth, a state of the system.

Lines of thought: Gramsci (hegemony), Mark Fisher ("Capitalist Realism"), critique of ideology.

Essay 04

Growth with nowhere to go

An economy that is only healthy when it grows, on a planet that does not grow with it — that is not an economic model, that is a bet against physics. We measure progress in percentage points and wonder why exhaustion keeps growing: of the soils, of the oceans, of the people.

Nobody disputes that growth can ease poverty. But at some point the relationship flips: then it is no longer prosperity that grows, only throughput — more production, more waste, more advertising explaining to us why we need what nobody missed yesterday.

A system that collapses when everyone buys only what they need does not have a consumption problem. It has a design problem.

The alternative is not renunciation as punishment but enough as a goal: shorter working hours instead of more stuff, public wealth instead of private excess, repair instead of replacement. That is not asceticism. That is the question of what all this is actually for.

Lines of thought: post-growth economics, Kate Raworth ("Doughnut Economics"), commons research.

Essay 05

Utopia is not a daydream. It is a duty.

We were taught to use "utopian" as an insult — as a synonym for naive. Yet the opposite is true: naive is whoever believes things can simply carry on. Utopia is not an escape. It is a tool: it measures the present against what would be possible, and makes visible what we put up with.

Every improvement we live in today was the utopia of the day before yesterday and the scandal of yesterday. Whoever abolishes utopia does not abolish dreams — only direction.

Realism that only describes what is, is not sobriety. It is the politest form of capitulation.

Which is why this page does not end with instructions but with a demand: imagine the world you actually want to live in — concretely, not as a mood. Then measure every headline, every excuse, every "that's just how it is" against it. At the beginning, no more nonconformism is needed than that.

Lines of thought: Bloch ("The Principle of Hope"), Adorno ("Wrong life cannot be lived rightly" — as a question), Solnit.
