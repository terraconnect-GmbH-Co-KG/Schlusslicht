Clean Energy Eclipse: Renewables Become Europe’s Energy Backbone

For the first time in history, wind and photovoltaic panels generated more electricity than fossil fuels across the European Union, a milestone experts call a major energy 'tipping point'[2].

In 2025, renewables produced 30% of the EU’s electricity, overtaking coal, oil, and gas at 29%, signaling that clean energy is no longer an alternative but the backbone of Europe’s power system[2]. By the first quarter of 2026, this share rose to 45.5%, according to Eurostat data[7].

Historic High Seas Treaty Protects 90% of Earth’s Living Ocean Space

After nearly two decades of negotiations, the Biodiversity Beyond National Jurisdiction (BBNJ) Agreement officially came into force, establishing legally binding protections for the high seas[2].

The treaty covers areas beyond national waters that make up over two-thirds of the ocean’s surface and more than 90% of Earth’s living space by volume, addressing a critical gap in global marine conservation[2]. It unites countries in defending ocean habitats that were previously largely unprotected[6].

Despite its historic scope, the treaty’s effectiveness depends on national implementation, funding for monitoring, and enforcement mechanisms in remote areas. Critics also note that without stronger measures against deep-sea mining and pollution, some risks to marine biodiversity may persist[2][5].

France’s PFAS Ban Ends a Century of 'Forever Chemical' Pollution

France officially banned products containing PFAS, known as 'forever chemicals' due to their extreme environmental persistence, in a landmark law affecting cosmetics, clothing, and ski wax[2].

The law prohibits the sale, production, or import of PFAS-containing products where safer alternatives already exist and mandates expanded drinking water testing[2]. It also opens pathways to hold polluters financially accountable, addressing decades of unchecked contamination[2].

PFAS are banned in cosmetics, clothing, and ski wax where alternatives exist[2]

The law mandates expanded testing of drinking water for PFAS contamination[2]

France is the first major economy to implement such a broad PFAS ban[2]

While the ban is a major step, challenges remain in tracking global supply chains and ensuring enforcement across imported goods. Some industries argue that safer alternatives are not yet available for all PFAS applications, potentially limiting the ban’s immediate scope[2].
