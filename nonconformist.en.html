Essay 01

The Algorithmic Gavel

Modern governance has quietly surrendered its political soul to the logic of efficiency, treating complex social choices as mere engineering puzzles. When leaders declare that a decision is 'data-driven' or 'technically necessary,' they imply that no legitimate alternative exists, silencing the very possibility of democratic debate.

Power hides in the spreadsheet; it does not wear a crown.

This shift transforms citizens from participants into subjects of management, whose needs are measured rather than heard. As Gabriella Syme and others note, the technocratic frame erases the normative dimension of politics, pretending that ethical questions about justice or freedom can be solved by algorithms.

To reclaim democracy, we must insist that governance is fundamentally about value, not just calculation. Only by refusing to let experts alone define the horizon of the possible can we restore the space where collective self-determination lives.

Lines of thought: Weber on bureaucracy, Simone Weil on attention, Foucault on technopolitics

Essay 02

The Engine of Discard

We are taught to see waste as a glitch in the system, a regrettable byproduct of overproduction that good management might fix. But capitalism does not merely produce waste; it depends on it, relying on the constant destruction of value to keep the cycle of accumulation moving.

Growth demands ruin; without discard, the machine starves.

This logic extends beyond factories to the social realm: people deemed 'obsolete' are cast away just as effortlessly as outdated products. The system's obsession with novelty masks its inability to sustain either ecology or community, replacing permanence with perpetual turnover.

A different economy must begin by refusing the mandate to discard, affirming durability and care over speed and novelty. Only then can we imagine a world where value is not measured by what we can throw away.

Lines of thought: Marx on waste of labor, Donna Haraway on chthulucene, Aimé Césaire on decolonization of value

Essay 03

The Privilege of Sight

Privacy is often celebrated as a universal right, a shield protecting individual freedom from the gaze of power. Yet in the capitalist order, it operates inversely: the rich buy invisibility to conceal abuse, while the poor are stripped of it, rendered hyper-visible to systems of control.

Invisibility is a fortress; visibility is a cage.

This asymmetry turns privacy into a marker of caste, where the ability to remain unseen is reserved for those who can afford it. Meanwhile, the marginalized face constant monitoring through welfare algorithms, workplace tracking, and predictive policing, their lives reduced to data points for extraction.

True freedom requires not just individual privacy but collective transparency, where power is exposed rather than concealed. Only by dismantling the class-based logic of the right to be unseen can we build a society where accountability serves everyone.

Lines of thought: Foucault on surveillance, Arendt on the public realm, Wendy Brown on privacy as property
