Essay 01

The Smoothness of Surrender

Convenience has been recast as a virtue, a neutral service that streamlines life and reduces effort. Yet under capitalism, it functions as a political mechanism that removes the friction necessary for critical thought and collective action [1].

When every obstacle is smoothed away, resistance loses its anchor; compliance becomes the only path because delay and struggle are economically punished.

This erosion of friction is not accidental but engineered, ensuring that the system operates without interruption by rendering dissent feel inefficient [2]. The promise of ease masks the cost of surrender, turning autonomy into a series of pre-selected options.

To reclaim freedom, we must reintroduce friction as a space for deliberation, refusing the smooth script that demands blind acquiescence to the logic of speed and efficiency.

Lines of thought: Byung-Chul Han, friction theory, automation as control

Essay 02

The End of Disagreement

Governance is increasingly delegated to algorithms that predict outcomes and optimize systems without human input. This shift replaces democratic deliberation with a form of predictive management that claims neutrality while enforcing specific values [1].

Disagreement is erased when politics is framed as data science; the algorithm does not argue, it calculates, making dissent appear as error rather than legitimate opposition.

The illusion of inevitability hides the political choices embedded in the code, delegating sovereignty to an elite that claims exclusive access to efficiency [2]. What was once a field of debate becomes a closed loop of optimization.

True democracy requires the capacity to disagree, to refuse the data-driven script, and to assert that human life cannot be reduced to variables in a predictive model.

Lines of thought: Algorithmic determinism, Hannah Arendt, critical data studies

Essay 03

The Tyranny of Speed

Efficiency is celebrated as the highest measure of progress, a neutral metric that maximizes output and minimizes cost. Yet it is a political imperative that demands the sacrifice of human complexity for the sake of systemic speed [1].

What cannot be measured is declared waste; the slow, the unquantifiable, the deeply human are rendered obsolete in the race for optimization.

This logic of speed erases the richness of life, turning every moment into a transaction that must yield value or be discarded [2]. The pursuit of efficiency becomes a form of violence against the unmeasurable aspects of existence.

To resist this tyranny, we must affirm the value of inefficiency, protecting the slow, the unclear, and the unquantifiable as essential to human dignity.

Lines of thought: Kathi Weeks, Byung-Chul Han, degrowth philosophy
