Essay 01

The Algorithmic Gaze

Technocracy has replaced the messy contest of values with the smooth certainty of data, presenting political decisions as mere calculations of optimal outcomes. When a society trusts algorithms over assemblies, it surrenders the right to disagree to the illusion of factual inevitability. This shift does not merely streamline governance; it redefines the citizen from a participant in power to a subject of management.

Efficiency is the new dogma; democracy is the old error.

The danger lies not in the tools themselves but in the refusal to acknowledge that every metric embeds a preference and every model enforces a worldview. By hiding normative choices behind the veil of neutrality, technocracy obscures the very power relations it claims to transcend. We are left with a system that solves problems we did not choose and ignores those we cannot measure.

To reclaim politics, we must reject the pretense that governance can be reduced to engineering and restore the space for conflict, judgment, and collective will. Only when we recognize that efficiency is a value, not a fact, can we begin to demand a system that serves human freedom rather than anometric order.

Lines of thought: Weber on bureaucracy, Simone Weil on attention, Foucault on technopolitics

Essay 02

The Economy of Disposability

In a system predicated on perpetual expansion, waste becomes the hidden engine of production, the inevitable byproduct of turning life into commodity. Every discarded smartphone, every expired contract, every unemployed body serves the logic of renewal that keeps the market moving[1]. This disposability is not accidental; it is the mechanism by which capitalism avoids confronting its own contradictions.

We do not consume things; we consume their endings.

The same logic that renders objects obsolete also renders people expendable, framing unemployment as obsolescence and poverty as personal failure[2]. By treating both labor and material as disposable, the system naturalizes inequality and evades responsibility for the human and ecological costs of its growth. Waste is the shadow of value, the proof that the system cannot sustain what it creates.

A politics of care must begin by refusing disposability, recognizing that the value of a thing or a person lies not in its exchange but in its endurance. To build a future beyond waste is to demand an economy that measures success by what it preserves, not by what it discards.

Lines of thought: Marx on waste of labor, Donna Haraway on chthulucene, Aimé Césaire on decolonization of value

Essay 03

The Fortress of the Self

The modern ideal of privacy has been hollowed out into a fortress of the self, protecting the wealthy from scrutiny while the poor remain perpetually visible to the state and the market. This asymmetry reveals that privacy is not a universal right but a commodity, distributed according to power and wealth[2]. When the rich can hide their offices and the poor cannot hide their bodies, privacy becomes a tool of hierarchy rather than liberation.

Privacy is the right to be invisible; exposure is the fate of the powerless.

Capitalism thrives on this divide, using surveillance to discipline workers while shielding corporate power behind layers of secrecy and legal privilege[1]. The result is a society where transparency is demanded from the bottom but forbidden at the top, reinforcing the very inequalities that democratic life should dismantle. To speak of privacy without addressing this imbalance is to speak only for the privileged.

True privacy must be reimagined as a collective right, a shared protection against the extractive gaze of power rather than a private refuge from accountability. Only when the right to be unseen is extended to all can we begin to build a world where freedom is not a privilege but a practice.

Lines of thought: Foucault on surveillance, Arendt on the public realm, Wendy Brown on privacy as property
