Essay 01

The Right to Banish

Private property is commonly mistaken for the right to use a resource, yet its true legal and political weight lies in the right to exclude others from it. This power of exclusion transforms shared environments into private fortresses, creating invisible boundaries that dictate who may exist where and how. Under capitalism, this exclusion is not accidental but endemic, structuring society into tiers of access and abandonment[1].

Property is not ownership; it is jurisdiction over silence. The owner does not hold the land; the land holds the owner's power to banish.

When businesses can lay off and exclude workers deemed 'obsolete,' they exercise a sovereignty that mirrors the arbitrary power of feudal lords. This dynamic denies 'projective autonomy' to those pushed out, forcing them into a state of permanent precariousness where their skills and lives are rendered disposable[1]. The system thus spawns grievances that cannot be solved by reform alone, because the fault lies in the very logic of exclusion itself[1].

To challenge this, the Left must integrate reform with revolution, limiting the right of exclusion while building movements that target capitalism as a whole. Only by dismantling the absolute right to banish can we restore the possibility of a world where resources serve life rather than enforce hierarchy[1].

Lines of thought: Left Libertarianism, Exclusion as Sovereignty, Projective Autonomy

Essay 02

The Moral Mask of Hierarchy

Meritocracy presents itself as a fair system of reward, where success flows naturally from talent and effort, yet it functions primarily as a moral justification for hierarchy. By framing inequality as the result of individual merit or failure, it masks the structural forces that determine who gets the chance to be 'meritorious' in the first place. This narrative convinces the disadvantaged that their position is a personal flaw rather than a political outcome, draining the energy needed for collective resistance.

Meritocracy is the alibi of the elite: it turns the crime of exclusion into a parade of fairness. The ladder is not broken; you were never meant to climb.

This system relies on the illusion that the starting lines are equal, ignoring how capital, education, and social networks are inherited rather than earned. When the Left critiques capitalism as a whole, it must attack this moral mask, revealing how the system spawns endemic faults that reward inheritance over human potential[1]. The critique of meritocracy is thus essential to any opposition that targets all the grievances the system spawns, not just the surface symptoms[1].

Dismantling meritocracy requires a program of 'projective autonomy' that guarantees equal initial resource entitlements independent of voluntary production. Only by rejecting the logic that rewards are solely the result of individual effort can we rebuild a society where power is not disguised as virtue[1].

Lines of thought: Meritocracy, Structural Inequality, Left Libertarianism

Essay 03

Democracy as Disruption

Modern democracy is often reduced to elections, parties, and procedures, as if participation alone guarantees freedom. Yet this formalism masks a deeper reality: democracy thrives not when it is managed, but when it disrupts the status quo [1].

Democracy is not the rule of the majority; it is the refusal of the powerless to be ruled.

When democracy becomes a tool for stabilizing hierarchy rather than exposing it, it ceases to be democratic. True democracy emerges in moments of collective action that refuse the limits imposed by elites and demand a reimagining of power itself [1].

To reclaim democracy is to recognize that its essence lies not in ballots but in the unyielding assertion that no one is entitled to rule without consent, and that consent must be continually renegotiated [1].

Lines of thought: Hannah Arendt, Jacques Rancière, John Dewey
